A reference for user researchers

Laws of Survey Design

Principles drawn from behavioural psychology and cognitive science that shape — and distort — responses in unmoderated surveys. Know the bias; design around it.

Response Biases

01 / automatic tendencies 4 principles

Acquiescence Bias

High impact

"Respondents tend to agree with whatever you put in front of them."

When uncertain or disengaged, respondents default to agreement regardless of content. Rooted in satisficing — conserving cognitive energy by selecting the first socially acceptable answer. Research suggests 10–20% of respondents exhibit this systematically.

How to reduce it

Use balanced scales with both positive and negative statements. Include reverse-coded items to detect satisficing patterns. Avoid exclusively positive-framed statement sets. Red herring items (contradictory pairs) can flag habitual agreers in analysis.

Social Desirability Bias

High impact

"People answer as the person they want to be, not the person they are."

Respondents give answers they believe will be viewed favourably by others — or by the imagined researcher. Particularly affects sensitive topics: usage frequency, effort invested, attitudes on contested subjects, and any self-evaluative question.

How to reduce it

State anonymity explicitly and credibly. Use third-person framing: "How often do people in your role…" instead of "How often do you…". Prefer open numeric input fields over predefined ranges — respondents anchor less to implied norms. Normalise the full range: "Some people do X, others do Y — what about you?"

Satisficing

High impact

"When cognitive cost rises, response quality falls."

Respondents select the first 'good enough' answer rather than their most accurate one when fatigued or overloaded. The Tourangeau, Rips & Rasinski cognitive model identifies satisficing as a core failure mode in survey response. It worsens over survey length, and with matrix grid questions in particular.

How to reduce it

Front-load your highest-priority questions. Keep total length short — each additional question has diminishing data quality. Vary question formats to maintain engagement. Show a progress indicator. Avoid matrix questions; break them into individual items.

Extreme & Central Tendency Bias

Medium impact

"Some always go to the edges. Others never leave the middle."

Cultural and individual differences cause systematic avoidance of either extreme or middle scale options — independent of true attitude. This makes cross-segment and cross-cultural Likert comparisons unreliable without calibration.

How to reduce it

Use forced-choice or ranking tasks for decisions where central tendency would undermine the insight. Validate scale responses with open-ended follow-ups. Exercise caution when comparing Likert data across cultures or demographic groups. Consider ipsative (comparative) scales for high-stakes attitudinal measurement.

Question Design

02 / how wording shapes answers 4 principles

Framing Effect

High impact

"The container shapes what you think is in it."

Logically equivalent questions produce different responses when framed positively versus negatively. "90% uptime" and "10% downtime" trigger different evaluations of identical facts. Question stems carry implicit evaluative weight that respondents absorb before they've formed a view.

How to reduce it

Test your key questions with both positive and negative frames before fielding. Use neutral, descriptive language in question stems. Strip evaluative adjectives. Where both frames are legitimate, consider split-sample testing and triangulating the responses.

Leading Questions

High impact

"The question already contains the answer you'll get."

Wording that implies a correct or expected answer primes respondents toward it before they've formed their own view. "How helpful was our support team?" assumes helpfulness. "Did you find the feature easy to use?" assumes engagement. The framing is the bias.

How to reduce it

Use neutral openers: "How would you describe…" rather than "How [positive adjective] was…". Have someone unfamiliar with the study hypothesis review every question for loaded language. Reverse-test: would you be equally comfortable if the question steered the opposite direction?

Hypothetical Bias

Medium impact

"What people say they would do and what they do are different data sets."

Responses to counterfactual or future-oriented questions ("Would you use this?", "Would you pay for that?") systematically overstate positive intent. Stated preference is not revealed preference. The gap is consistent and well-documented across product research, economics, and environmental valuation.

How to reduce it

Anchor to past behaviour wherever possible: "When did you last…" rather than "Would you…". Use behavioural proxies over direct intent questions. Treat hypothetical data as directional signal, not predictive measurement. Flag clearly in reporting that stated-preference data carries this known inflation.

Double-Barrelled Questions

Medium impact

"One question. One thing. Always."

Questions that ask about two things simultaneously — "How easy and enjoyable was the experience?" — produce uninterpretable answers. A respondent who found it easy but tedious has no valid option. The data looks clean but is structurally ambiguous.

How to reduce it

Read each question aloud and count how many distinct things it asks. If more than one: split it. Watch for conjunctions ('and', 'or') in question stems — they're a reliable double-barrel signal. This applies equally to scale items and qualitative prompts.

Order & Context Effects

03 / sequence matters 3 principles

Question Order Effect

High impact

"Earlier questions rewrite the cognitive context for later ones."

Asking about overall satisfaction before specific features contaminates both readings — they are no longer independent measures. Prior questions prime cognitive frameworks that shape how subsequent questions are interpreted, not just answered.

How to reduce it

Follow the general-to-specific rule: overall questions before specific ones. Separate attitudinal and behavioural questions. Where priming is a real concern, randomise question order and check for order effects in analysis. Never put competitor or category questions immediately before your product questions.

Anchoring Effect

High impact

"The first number seen becomes the floor or ceiling for every number after."

Numeric estimates are disproportionately influenced by the first value encountered. Scale endpoints define the range of what feels 'reasonable'. A scale that runs 0–10 elicits different distributions than one running 1–7 for the same underlying construct — both in absolute values and in discriminative power.

How to reduce it

Choose scale endpoints from the actual expected distribution of responses, not convention. Avoid starting numeric scales at dramatic extremes. Pilot specifically to check whether the range itself is influencing where responses cluster. Label scale endpoints with explicit behavioural anchors, not just numbers.

Response Option Order Effect

Medium impact

"First and last options are not equal to the ones in between."

In visual surveys, the first option benefits from primacy bias. In long or verbal lists, the last benefits from recency. Position systematically influences selection independent of meaning — a well-placed mediocre option will outperform a poorly-placed strong one.

How to reduce it

Randomise response options where the order carries no inherent meaning. For ordinal scales (agree–disagree, satisfaction), maintain a consistent direction throughout the survey — switching direction mid-survey increases error. Test both ascending and descending scale directions if validity is critical.

Cognitive & Memory Effects

04 / limits of recall 4 principles

Cognitive Load & Survey Fatigue

High impact

"Every question borrows from a finite attention budget."

Response quality degrades over survey length. Questions later in a survey receive less thoughtful answers even when respondents are still completing it — this isn't dropout, it's silent quality decay. Complex matrix grids are disproportionately expensive and accelerate fatigue.

How to reduce it

Apply Miller's Law: respect the 7±2 cognitive chunks constraint. Avoid matrix grids — decompose them into individual questions. Use branching logic to eliminate questions irrelevant to a given respondent. Show a progress indicator. Put your most critical questions first. Every question you add should earn its place.

Telescoping Effect

Medium impact

"Memory compresses time. Recent events feel older; vivid events feel recent."

Respondents systematically misplace events in time. Recent mundane events get pushed further back (forward telescoping); salient or emotional events get pulled closer (backward telescoping). Any recall-based question — frequency, last occurrence, duration — is affected.

How to reduce it

Use bounded, specific recall windows: "in the last 7 days" not "recently". Provide temporal landmarks to help respondents orient ("since the last update / since Christmas / since you joined"). Avoid recall windows longer than 30 days for non-salient routine behaviours — the data will not be reliable.

Demand Characteristics

Medium impact

"Respondents are detectives trying to figure out what you want."

When participants can infer the study's hypothesis or the researcher's desired outcome, they adjust their answers accordingly — either to please the researcher or to subvert them. Particularly problematic when the product under evaluation is obvious, and the survey context signals its origin.

How to reduce it

Obscure the specific hypothesis where ethically and practically possible. Include decoy questions that signal a broader study scope. Avoid survey titles and introductory text that signal the desired outcome. Use neutral third-party survey distribution where brand association is a concern.

Peak-End Rule

Medium impact

"People remember the worst moment, the best moment, and the last one."

Evaluation of an experience is dominated by its emotional peak and its final moment — not a faithful average. Post-experience surveys therefore capture the memory of an experience, not the experience itself. The gap matters most for journeys with high emotional variance.

How to reduce it

Be explicit about the evaluation window you want ("thinking about your experience this week" vs "overall to date"). For journeys with high emotional variance, consider in-moment surveys over retrospective ones. Design the end of your survey deliberately — the final question shapes how the whole interaction is remembered and rated. This card is last for a reason.

Further reading

These are the shoulders this stands on. Each is worth time if you want to go deeper.

Foundational texts

  • The Psychology of Survey Response Tourangeau, Rips & Rasinski · Cambridge University Press, 2000 The definitive cognitive model of how respondents comprehend, retrieve, judge, and report answers. The intellectual foundation for most of what's on this page.
  • Question and Questionnaire Design Krosnick & Presser · Cambridge Handbook of Research Methods, 2024 Comprehensive coverage of open vs. closed questions, scale design, acquiescence, response order, social desirability, and questionnaire length — with synthesised best practices.
  • Survey Research Krosnick · Annual Review of Psychology, 1999 Influential review paper covering the satisficing model of survey response — essential reading for understanding why response quality degrades over length and complexity.

Practitioner references

  • GESIS Survey Guidelines GESIS – Leibniz Institute for the Social Sciences The most rigorous free practitioner reference available. Academic in tone but comprehensive — covers acquiescence, social desirability, response order effects, scale design, and more.
  • Writing Survey Questions Pew Research Center Pew's methodology team publishes their reasoning publicly. Pragmatic, well-grounded guidance on question wording, response scales, and avoiding common design errors.
  • 106 Cognitive Biases & Principles growth.design Not survey-specific, but the closest thing to a visual, scannable reference for cognitive biases in designed interactions. Covers many biases directly relevant to survey response.

Design inspiration

  • Laws of UX Jon Yablonski The direct inspiration for this site — psychological principles made accessible for practitioners. The format, philosophy, and meta-application of its own principles to this page's structure are all borrowed from here.
